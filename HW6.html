rwb <- colorRampPalette(c("red","white","black"))
stocks <- data.frame(descriptive=c("atrocious","terrible","bad","neutral","good","great","fantastic"),
                     rating=c(-30,-20,-10,0,10,20,30))
stocks$descriptive <- factor(stocks$descriptive,levels=stocks$descriptive)
barplot(rating~descriptive,data=stocks,col=rwb(7), ylab="Change in Stock Value (%)", xlab="Descriptive Change in Stock Value", main="Descriptive Stock Value Label vs. Change in Stock Value (%)")

I used a divergent pallette to color a barplot that detailing change in stock value. The neutral line of no change is 0% and is white. The darker the color black the larger the increase in stock value percentagewise; conversely, the darker the color red the larger the decrease in stock value percentagewise. This captures the divergent color pallette becoming either darker black or darker red.

Relative scaling can be used to highlight the numeric amount of change from column to column in a bar chart. In general, relative scaling puts a spotlight on the numeric amount of change from osbervation to observation (or column to column/data point to data point). Conversely, absolute scaling better identifies the proportional change frome column to column in a bar chart. In general, absolute scaling does a better job of giving an intuitive/visual sense of the proportional change from observation to observation (or column to column/data point to data point).
